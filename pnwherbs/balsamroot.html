  • Arrow-Leaved Balsamroot

    Balsamorhiza sagittata

    COMMON NAMES: Arrowleaf balsamroot, Spring sunflower, Oregon sunflower

    FAMILY: Asteraceae

    NATIVE RANGE: Western North America — especially in dry mountain meadows, sagebrush hillsides, and forest clearings

    EDIBILITY:

    This plant was a vital traditional food of many Indigenous tribes, offering nourishment from nearly every part.

    • Roots: Can be steamed, roasted, or dried and ground. Strong, piney taste—nutritious but resinous.
    • Seeds: Rich in oil and protein. Can be roasted, eaten raw, or pressed for oil.
    • Young Leaves: Tender leaves may be eaten raw or cooked like spinach; older ones are fibrous.
    • Stalks: Peeled and eaten fresh; similar to celery.

    PREPARATION:

    • Roots: Long roasting or slow cooking helps break down bitterness.
    • Seeds: Dry-roast or mash into meal.
    • Leaves: Best harvested young in spring and lightly cooked.

    MEDICINAL USES:

    A powerful herb with a long-standing role in Western native herbalism, especially among Plateau and Great Basin peoples.

    Traditional Herbal Applications:

    • Respiratory Remedy: Decoction from roots treats coughs, chest congestion, bronchitis, and tuberculosis.
    • Antiseptic: Fresh root chewed or poulticed for infections, wounds, or swollen lymph nodes.
    • Anti-inflammatory: Applied externally to sore joints, sprains, and inflamed tissues.
    • Immune Support: Used as a general immune stimulant during illness or seasonal transition.

      • Its strong, resinous root is similar in effect to echinacea or osha — helping clear mucus, fight infection, and renew vigor.

    OTHER USES:

    • Salve Ingredient: Often used in healing salves and infused oils.
    • Ceremonial Smudge: Dried roots or leaves used in spiritual cleansing smoke.
    • Survival Resource: Highly valued in times of scarcity — both food and medicine in one.

    IDENTIFICATION:

    • Leaves: Large, arrowhead-shaped, silver-green, velvety, and fuzzy. Grow in basal rosettes.
    • Flowers: Bright yellow, daisy-like blooms with long petals, resembling miniature sunflowers.
    • Roots: Thick, woody, and aromatic. Very sticky when broken.
    • Growth Habit: Low-growing perennial with one tall flower stalk per plant.
