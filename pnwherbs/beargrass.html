  • Beargrass

    Xerophyllum tenax

    COMMON NAMES: Beargrass, Indian Basket Grass, Soap Grass

    FAMILY: Melanthiaceae

    NATIVE RANGE: Western North America — subalpine meadows, open forests, and mountainsides of the Pacific Northwest and Northern Rockies

    EDIBILITY:

    Beargrass is not typically consumed due to its tough and fibrous leaves and low nutritional value. However, its utility lies in survival and ceremonial uses.

    Edibility Notes:

    • Leaves: Not edible, but extremely durable.
    • Roots: Occasionally used historically in emergency survival, but generally not recommended for ingestion.
    • Flowers: Some tribes may have used flowers or seeds sparingly, but not for sustenance.

      • While named Beargrass, it's not a true grass. Bears have been observed using it for bedding and occasionally foraging among its habitat, but not feeding heavily on it.

    PREPARATION:

    Used primarily in traditional Indigenous medicine for external applications and practical utility.

    MEDICINAL USES:

    Traditional Herbal Applications:

    • Wound Dressing: Soft inner leaf fibers or flower stems applied to cuts or scrapes.
    • Anti-Septic WashDecoction of roots or leaves used externally to clean wounds or soothe irritation.
    • Burn Poultice: Used in combination with other herbs for burn care.
    • Respiratory Steam: Infused plant used in steam tents to open sinuses and lungs.

    OTHER USES:

    • Basketry: Highly valued by Indigenous peoples (e.g., Salish, Kootenai, and Blackfeet) for weaving baskets, ropes, mats, and ceremonial regalia.
    • Cordage:Leaves twisted into strong threads or ropes for binding tools, bundles, or even fishing nets.Leaves twisted into strong threads or ropes for binding tools, bundles, or even fishing nets.
    • Fire Starter: Dried leaves ignite easily and were used as tinder.

      • Cultural Note: The harvesting and preparation of Beargrass for basketry is considered a sacred art and is often accompanied by prayer or ritual.

    IDENTIFICATION:

    • Leaves: Long, narrow, tough, and grass-like; grow in dense clumps from a basal rosette.
    • Flowers: Tall (3–6 feet), unbranched, bearing a cone-shaped cluster of small white flowers.
    • Flower Stalk: Star-shaped and fragrant, blooming in early to mid-summer.
    • Habitat: Mountain slopes, open forests, and meadows — often after fire disturbance.
