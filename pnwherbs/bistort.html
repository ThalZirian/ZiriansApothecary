    Bistort spp.

    COMMON NAMES: Common bistort, Snakeweed, Easter giant

    FAMILY: Polygonaceae

    NATIVE RANGE: Temperate regions of the Northern Hemisphere â€“ moist meadows, streambanks, and woodlands

    EDIBILITY:

    • Leaves: Young leaves edible; added to soups or stews (traditionally in Easter Lenten meals).
    • Roots: Astringent and starchy; boiled or roasted (more commonly medicinal).
    • Flowers: Occasionally used as garnish or mild infusion.

    PREPARATION:

    • Roots are decocted or powdered for internal and external use.
    • Leaves are used fresh or blanched in recipes.
    • Dried aerial parts used in traditional herbal blends.

    MEDICINAL USES:

    • Strong astringent (controls bleeding, diarrhea)
    • Gargled for sore throat or inflamed gums
    • Applied externally to wounds and insect bites
    • Supports digestive healing and ulcers

    OTHER USES:

    • Red dye source from roots
    • Ritual herb in European seasonal celebrations
    • Folk use in protective charms and boundary spells

    IDENTIFICATION:

    • Leaves: Broad, arrowhead or lance-shaped; basal rosette
    • Flower: Spikes of pinkish to purplish flower clusters
    • Stem: Erect, often reddish; unbranched or sparsely branched
    • Root: Thick, twisted rhizomes; reddish-brown, rich in tannins
