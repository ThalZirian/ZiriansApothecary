    Bitterroot spp.

    COMMON NAMES: Hairy Bitterroot, Pennsylvania Bitterroot, Ladyâ€™s Smock

    FAMILY: Montiaceae

    NATIVE RANGE: Rocky mountain slopes, dry open forests, and sagebrush deserts of western North America

    EDIBILITY:

    • Roots: Traditionally consumed by Native American tribes; peeled and boiled or steamed.
    • Bitterness: Intense unless processed or harvested before blooming.
    • Note: Highly revered as a sacred food; not typically for casual foraging.

    PREPARATION:

    • Root is peeled and soaked to reduce bitterness before cooking.
    • Traditionally steamed in earth ovens or boiled in clay pots.
    • Sometimes dried and rehydrated for later ceremonial use.

    MEDICINAL USES:

    • Promotes digestive tract resilience and cleansing
    • Used ceremonially to strengthen spiritual resolve
    • Mild sedative properties when steeped (less common use)
    • Regarded as an emotional reset herb in indigenous practices

    OTHER USES:

    • Sacred medicine among Salish and Plateau tribes
    • Used in ceremonies of renewal and rebirth
    • State flower of Montana; symbol of resilience and survival

    IDENTIFICATION:

    • Leaves: Narrow, fleshy, and basal; disappear by flowering
    • Flower: Large, showy pink to white petals with many stamens
    • Stem: Very short or absent at flowering time
    • Root: Thick, fleshy taproot; reddish-brown and twisted

    • Element: Earth + Spirit
